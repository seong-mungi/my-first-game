# George Fan

## Identity

- **Role:** Game designer
- **Notable work:** Designed Plants vs. Zombies (2009) at PopCap Games
- **Post-PopCap:** Founded All Yes Good; designed *Octogeddon* (2018)

## Why He Matters Here

George Fan is the **architect of the lane defense subgenre**. His design decisions on PvZ established the genre conventions that hold to this day:

- 5-row lane structure
- Grid-cell placement (one plant per cell)
- Sun economy with generator units
- Loadout cap (10 plants from 49)
- Environment-as-mechanical-modifier (pool, roof, fog)

(Source: [[Wikipedia Plants vs Zombies]])

## Documented Design Rationale

Fan explicitly described his lane choice as a critique of existing tower defense:

> "Enemies in tower defense games would never attack the towers" (paraphrased; Source: [[Wikipedia Plants vs Zombies]])

This was the design problem he set out to fix. His solution — parallel lanes with grid placement — let zombies and plants engage directly, which he saw as more intuitive.

He also intentionally **named units after their function** ("Peashooter shoots peas, Wall-nut is a wall") to leverage player intuition rather than tutorial overhead. This is a documented accessibility-first design principle.

## Lessons For This Project

- **Find the ergonomic wrong** in your reference genre. Fan's "enemies never attack towers" was the productive friction. What's the analogous friction in your reference genre?
- **Functional naming** is a huge accessibility lever. If a player can read a unit name and predict its behavior, you've saved a paragraph of tutorial.
- **Constraint-driven design.** The 10-plant loadout cap was a constraint, not a feature. The constraint *created* the meta-game.
